JETS, HULLS, HARDWARE & ORDERING
Why should I have a Wooldridge jet tunnel?
Our tunnel will get you farther up river with less potential for sliding into an obstacle or hitting your jet foot intake.
What type of anode should I have on my saltwater aluminum boat?
Sacrificial anodes come in three materials: zinc, magnesium, and aluminum. Use zinc if your boat lives only in salt water. If you run both fresh and salt, use an aluminum anode. Fresh water only — magnesium.
What style liner / wear-ring do I need?
There are two types: flanged liners and flangeless liners (also known as wear-rings). Flanged liners have a flange with six mounting holes around the circumference; flangeless liners are held in place with two or four bolts that mount the liner directly to the jet foot.
Do you repair outboard motors at the factory?
We do not service or repair outboard or inboard engines at the factory, though we're happy to help where we can or direct you to a service center in your area.
What is the advantage of a two-piece windshield?
We developed the two-piece windshield for better visibility and easier beach access. Two larger pieces of glass are easier to see through in poor conditions than three smaller pieces with an extra divider — and the door is much larger, so getting gear (or a less-mobile passenger) through the bow is far easier. The smaller the boat, the more you notice it.
Do all square or flat-bow boats have flat bottoms?
No. We don't build any boats with a flat bottom, though some folks assume so from the shape of the nose. The least deadrise we put in any hull is 9 degrees.
What is the advantage of a square or flat bow?
In many cases there's more square footage in the boat and bow area, and the windshield can move farther forward than on a pointed-nose boat. Many folks also find it easier to load gear and people through the bow when pulled up on a bank or beach. Generally there's less deadrise in the bow of a square-nose boat than a pointed-nose boat.
What determines the ride of a boat?
Weight, length, bottom shape, deadrise, and where you're positioned in the boat all determine how it feels in different water conditions.
